# Case Study: The School That Almost Ran Out of Lunch

**Created:** 2026-03-20
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-20
**Context:** Illustrates how the PB&J Machine Co. batch scheduling interface removes operational overhead from cafeteria managers and eliminates supply-side failure modes.

## 📋 Summary

Riverside Elementary feeds 340 kids every day. For three years, that meant a cafeteria manager manually estimating weekly sandwich counts, hedging against calendar uncertainty, and hoping the numbers came out close enough. One Thursday they didn't. A last-minute district calendar change brought 80 more kids than sandwiches. This case study follows what changed when PB&J Machine Co. replaced that manual process — and what it meant for the person doing the work.

## 👣 What Donna's Week Looked Like

### Before

```
Monday morning: Donna estimates attendance for the week
  → Adjusts for field trips, half days, known absences
  → Places sandwich order based on the estimate

Tuesday–Thursday: Order arrives in batches
  → Donna monitors inventory against actual attendance
  → Adjusts manually if numbers diverge

Thursday (October): District adds a makeup day
  → Donna didn't know until that morning
  → 80 more kids than sandwiches
  → Some kids doubled up on sides
  → A few parents were called
```

### After

```
One-time setup: Donna configures weekly templates in the batch interface
  → 340 sandwiches, white bread, grape jelly, Mon–Wed
  → 300 sandwiches, wheat, strawberry, Thu (art day, late lunch)
  → 360 sandwiches, white bread, grape jelly, Fri (Fridays always run over)

Every morning: Machine produces exactly what the schedule calls for
  → Packaged and labeled, ready before the lunch line opens
  → No estimation, no manual ordering

District pushes a calendar change: Schedule updates automatically
  → Donna adjusts the count in the same interface she already uses
  → Machine recalibrates before 7 AM
```

## 🔍 The Situation

Donna had been running Riverside's cafeteria lunch program for three years. The Thursday incident wasn't dramatic — kids got fed, nobody went without — but it exposed a structural problem. The process had a single point of failure: Donna's estimate. If the estimate was off, the math was off. The system had no way to absorb a surprise.

What made it harder was that the surprise wasn't unusual. District calendars shift. Field trips get rescheduled. Half days appear. Every one of those events was a variable that Donna had to track manually and factor into a Monday morning number. Most weeks she got it right. But getting it right required ongoing effort that had nothing to do with running a cafeteria.

## 🔄 What Changed

Riverside's district deployed PB&J Machine Co. that January. Donna accessed the batch scheduling interface through her existing cafeteria management platform — same login, no new system to learn. She set up her weekly templates once: counts, bread types, jelly flavors, days. From that point forward, the machine produced what the schedule said, every morning, before she arrived.

The calendar-change problem went away structurally. When the district updated the school calendar, the schedule updated automatically. When Donna needed to adjust a count, she did it in the same interface she used for everything else, and the machine recalibrated overnight. The estimate was gone. The variable was gone.

## ✅ The Outcome

Donna hasn't manually counted sandwich orders since February.

The Thursday incident can't happen anymore — not because Donna got better at estimating, but because estimating is no longer part of the process. The machine produces what it's told from nothing. The only number Donna manages is the count, and now the system helps her manage that too.

When asked what the product changed for her, Donna said something that didn't make it into the press release: "I used to think about lunch on Sunday nights. I don't anymore."

## 💡 What This Illustrates

The problem was never that Donna was bad at her job. She was good at it — she knew her kids, her staff, her kitchen. The problem was that the job included work that shouldn't have been her work: maintaining a mental model of a variable system and converting it into a number every Monday morning.

That's what the product took away. Not the cafeteria work. The overhead that had accumulated around it.

The machine doesn't run short because it doesn't have a supply chain to get caught in. The schedule doesn't break because it's not a spreadsheet — it's a live configuration connected to a system that acts on it. What PB&J Machine Co. returned to Donna was the job she was actually hired to do.
